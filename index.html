Too many tabs opened?

August 22, 2013

Have you ever had so many tabs opened that favicons start dissapearing?

Or wait eternal minutes to recover from a browser crash?

And then try to find from what tab the sound is coming from?

If you experienced one of those things, welcome to the "too many tabs" club. Do not worry, some members have over 300 tabs opened. Outsiders cannot understand how we can manage all those tabs at the same time, they think there is a serious problem with us.

The truth? We are not managing all those tabs, we are just piling them up.

It is clear that tabs perform an important role in the way we navigate today. I got so used to them that is hard to remember those days when browsers did not implemented tab functionality. I tend to abuse the "Open in New Tab" option.

However, when I take a detailed look at my browser tabs, I find that most of them are things that I am not using right now and I do not even want to bookmark them for the future (takes time and they get mixed with my bookmarks). I just want to check them later. That means that I may need them later to work, to share with my friends or to read during my free time.

Of course there are tabs that I like to have constantly opened like Gmail, but from my current 98 opened tabs, I only need 5 right now. That is only a 5%!

If I had a way to freeze the tabs I do not use, I could free some memory.

That is why my partner and I are building Listboard.it.

With Listboard.it, you keep your browser(s) in sync on real time and easily drag windows or tabs to a later listboard. After dragging a tab, it will automatically close. Then you can recover those windows/tabs whenever you want.

Are you worried about losing the tab context? Trust me, you can always get the context back. It does not justify keeping the tab opened.

You can also bookmark tabs by just dragging them to a future listboard and organize them using hierarchical lists. Also, Listboard.it understands that the 3 steps of bookmarking do not always happen at the same time:

  • Open an URL
  • Analyze the content
  • Save it
Step 2 can happen a day after step 1.

So if are part of the club, want to free some memory and agree that bookmarking is not the solution, feel free to register for early testing or share your comment on HN.

I would love to hear your opinion.

China hits Listboard.it

July 24, 2013

A couple of weeks ago, I noticed that suddenly many users registered to Listboard.it. First, I thought that it was some sort of spam, but later, site analytics showed me that they were all unique visitors coming mostly from China thanks to an article from oschina.net that referred our site product.

Google translator was a big help here as I know zero Chinese. It helped me dig deeper and realized that it was not only an article about Listboard.it, but one of my old posts translated to Chinese.

It said almost the same except for one part that called my attention. I wrote that I used Google Drive to manage large documents while they wrote that I use Dropbox. Immediately, I recalled why the did that. Google Drive is blocked on China.

They also added some sort of picture that was very commented on the site. Not exactly the way I work, but plays well with the article.

It was a very interesting finding and, well, I cannot complain as Listboard.it got over 200 subscriptions thanks to it!

Co-developing over 8,300 miles of distance

June 15, 2013

Every time I tell my friends from Argentina that I am working on Listboard.it with a developer from Russia, they ask me "How do you do it?" It is clear why they ask. Denis and I are over 8,300 miles of distance (over 13,300 km), we have our full time jobs and, even worse, we are in completely different time zones. Luckily for us, "developers developed great developing tools". What I mean is that current applications to manage and coordinate development are amazing. They make our life so much easier.

Whether you are working 2 blocks or thousands of miles away, there is almost no difference. Distance is not a big deal thanks to Internet. That is why all the data we generate for our project (code, notes, documents, stylesheets, pictures, etc) is always kept on the Web. Our tools have access to the Internet and some are directly used from a browser.

Maybe, the big challenge is having different time zones. We both have our full time jobs and when I have free time to work on Listboard.it (6pm to 11pm my time), Denis is sleeping (1 am to 5am his time). Same problem when he codes. We never work both at the same time on the project and yet we manage to perfectly coordinate. We know what each of us is working on, our code is always synced, we have an active communication and we never block each other's work.

How do we do that? Well, it is not magic, but only 5 awesome tools. I will not get into much detail on how they work, just mention them:

  • Trello: maybe the most important one; we use it to coordinate future, current and finished tasks.
  • Google drive: helps us save and share large documents.
  • Bitbucket (git): keeps our code synced, lets us do code review and manage branches per task.
  • Email: we use it to send updates on what we have worked on or when there is something important to notify the other developer about.
  • Skype: although we do not use it that much, whenever we have something that will be too long to discuss by email, we chat 10 minutes on our free time.

I have to admit that it helps a lot that Denis is a great person and we have never had any problem. We both love what we do and are trying to build an excellent application. If you are interested on knowing more about our product or becoming an early tester, feel free to register.

Bookmarking is much more complex than you think

May 17, 2013

Long time ago I decided to work on a bookmarking site as a side project. If you are thinking "not another bookmarking site please", that is good! It means that you know there are constant new attempts to save/organize/share bookmarks that keep failing. But you may also know that there is still an unsatisfied market.

Let me explain why I picked this side project.

As many people out there, I constantly deal with the three categories of bookmarking: Now, Later, and Future. For those who never heard about those categories, here is a quick summary:

  • "Now" is for URLs that you are using right now such as research URLs on your current project, Gmail, etc. (Yes, all those tabs that you have now open!);
  • "Later" category represents what you find interesting enough to take a deeper look later when you have more time available.
  • "Future" are the bookmarks you want to archive/organize to access someday in the long future.

My problem is that I have to use different apps for each category. That makes it really difficult to smoothly move a bookmark from one category to another. If I use Pinboard for later and future bookmarks, I still end up with many opened tabs for what I am working right now.

I have been long waiting for a solution that properly handles all three of them. But there is none so far (at least that I know).

Why is that? We get around two to three new bookmarking sites each month. Why most of them fail? Why some of us are still looking for a way to save their bookmarks?

Even despite anyone trying to create a bookmarking site can find what the "not-yet-satisfied" users want. It is easy. Just perform a quick search and tons of comment and feedback will appear. Here are some from Hacker News:

  • "I mainly want this so that I can organize them (delete, properly name, putting them into different folders, etc) and download them as html."
  • "Apart from cross device availability, please make collaboration easier. Many times I bookmark pages for later market research or sharing it with my coworkers"
  • "... really simple and good search, RSS for tags, syncing with my bookmarks on all the browsers"
  • "... letting me save my bookmarks to a bookmark file readable by Firefox"
  • "They have to shown thats it's really really easy before they become converts. "
  • "I can't be bothered with tagging or confirmation dialogs. I just want to press a single button and have the browser remember it."
  • "I wish there was a program that let you import HTML/JSON files into it from FF, IE, Chrome, Safari, etc then had some good organization features almost exactly like firefox. It should also have the ability to delete duplicate links, organize links, put tags, create folders, etc."
  • "I really want a private bookmarking thing that will: i) Import all my bookmarks. ii) Gradually crawl images for all those bookmarks. iii) Display everything in a pinterest style grid iv) allow me to drag and drop links around; to add dividers; etc v) allow me to add tags or descriptions etc."

I came to the conclution that bookmarking proved to be hard. It is not just about saving some URLs. It is so much more than that. Way more complex than we think it is.

If some new app wants to get that remaining market, something is clear, it has to be disruptive.

Bitcoins in Argentina

April 23, 2013

I read about Bitcoin back in 2011, but it was not until this year that I got really involved with it. For those who never heard about Bitcoin, it is just a new currency, like dollars or euros. Some people describe it as a 'digital currency' or 'crypto-currency', but I prefer to use only the term 'currency' to ease its adoption as suggested in this excellent article by Jonathan Stacke.

Without going into much details on how it works (I look forward to explain it in future posts), let just say that it operates thanks to the collective power of many connected computers. If you want more details right away, visit Bitcoin's official site or check this video.

The great thing about Bitcoin is that it is not regulated by any central authority. You can transfer money directly to another person with your computer or mobile. Wow! A non-regulated currency! ... wait, won´t that scare people? No. At least not here in Argentina where we know about regulations. Our goverment regulates the amount of dollars you can buy (regulates, not prohibit), so Bitcoin raises as an insteresting alternative currency.

One week ago, Bitcoinfilm.org created a documentary about Bitcoins in Argentina.

Here it is:


This video made its way to a major Argentinian newspaper (spanish) and I noticed that many friends starting talking about it, asking questions mostly. They were certainly interested in Bitcoin. Besides that, around 100 people assist to last Bitcoin MeetUp in Buenos Aires and it is not crazy to expect that more people will assist to future ones.

Those are all positive signs. You can tell that Argentina is slowly adopting it. There is still a long path down the road for Bitcoins, but it worth following it.
